1Introduction & Scope

This document presents the architecture and design for Utility International’s self-service billing system — a turnkey Java EE solution that lets customers view, dispute and pay their electricity, gas and water bills online.

The design realises the supplied business domain model and the five use cases (Calculate Bill, View Bill, Dispute Bill, Resolve Disputed Bill, Pay Bill). It integrates with two existing external systems — the recently-installed billing system (accessed via web services) and the third-party payment clearing house (exposed as a JMS queue) — and is structured to accommodate the planned push-notification enhancement without rework.

The accompanying implementation is a layered JPA codebase. The persistence model deliberately uses two distinct inheritance strategies: a @MappedSuperclass (BaseEntity) to share identity and audit columns, and JPA single-table @Inheritance for the MonthlyBill → Gas/Water/Electricity hierarchy. A Data Access Object (DAO) layer encapsulates persistence, and a Data Transfer Object (DTO) layer isolates the presentation tier from the entities.

3Component Diagram

The component view shows the major Java EE building blocks and how they interact across tiers. The web tier (JSP + a controller servlet) drives session EJBs in the business tier; those EJBs use DAO components over JPA for persistence and integrate outbound with the external billing web service and the JMS payment queue. A provided interface (lollipop) and required interface (socket) notation shows the dependency direction.

4Deployment Diagram

The deployment view describes the physical tiers. Clients reach a load-balanced pair of Java EE application server nodes (web + EJB containers co-located) over HTTPS; the app servers connect to a clustered relational database and reach the external billing and payment systems over the corporate network.

5Sequence Diagram — Calculate Bill

The billing system is invoked externally (scheduled or manual). For each customer it gathers usage and pricing, the system sums the line costs, and the generated bill is forwarded to the customer’s workspace.

6Sequence Diagram — View Bill

The customer logs in, the application loads the workspace (their bills as DTOs), and the customer selects and reviews the most recent bill before proceeding to pay.

7Sequence Diagram — Dispute Bill

The customer rejects a bill within the 30-day window and supplies supporting text. The system checks the bill is still disputable, records the note, sets the status to DISPUTED, and forwards it to a call-centre agent’s workspace.

8Sequence Diagram — Resolve Disputed Bill

A call-centre agent reviews a disputed bill and either upholds the dispute (status RESOLVED) or rejects it and returns the bill to the customer for payment unchanged (status REJECTED_DISPUTE).

9Sequence Diagram — Pay Bill

The customer selects a bill due for payment and supplies card details. The system records a Payment, forwards the payment to the external clearing house (JMS), and marks the bill PAID.

10Top Technical Risks & Mitigations

The three risks below carry the most material impact on the project. Each is rated for severity and paired with a concrete mitigation strategy.

Risk Severity Description Mitigation Strategy
Coupling to the external billing web service HIGH Bills are produced by a separately-owned billing system reached over web services. Its availability, latency and contract (WSDL/schema) changes are outside our control, and any outage or breaking change directly blocks the core View/Calculate flows. Introduce an anti-corruption / adapter layer that isolates the external contract behind our own interface, add a circuit breaker with timeouts and graceful degradation, and cache the most recently generated bills so customers can still view (if not regenerate) during a billing-system outage. Version the adapter against the published WSDL and add contract tests.
Third-party payment gateway (JMS) reliability HIGH Payments are handled by a third party exposed as a JMS queue. Lost, duplicated or delayed messages risk double-charging, unconfirmed payments, or bills wrongly left outstanding — a direct financial and trust impact. Treat payment as asynchronous and idempotent: attach a unique payment reference so retries cannot double-charge, configure a dead-letter queue for poison messages, and run a scheduled reconciliation job that compares local Payment records against clearing-house settlement reports. Persist payment intent locally before enqueueing.
Concurrency & data integrity on bill state MEDIUM Self-service load means the same bill may be acted on concurrently (e.g. a customer pays while an agent resolves a dispute), risking lost updates and invalid status transitions such as paying an already-disputed bill. Use optimistic locking — the @Version column already present on BaseEntity — to detect concurrent edits, and enforce a bill status state-machine in the service layer so only legal transitions (e.g. OUTSTANDING→DISPUTED, DISPUTED→RESOLVED) are permitted. Wrap each use case in a single transaction.

11Assumptions

The following assumptions had a material impact on the architecture and design decisions:

  • Authentication & identity are provided by an existing corporate IAM / single sign-on facility; the self-service application consumes an authenticated customer identity rather than managing credentials itself.
  • Bill generation and discounting are owned by the external billing system. Our calculateBill logic models the summation of usage line-items for design completeness, but the authoritative figures (including discounts and special charges) originate from the billing web service.
  • One operating market context per customer: a customer’s bills are denominated in a single currency and governed by one regulatory regime at a time, even though Utility International operates across North America and Europe.
  • The tariff-change capability (changing electricity/gas/water packages) updates the customer’s pricing for future billing periods; recalculation of issued bills remains the billing system’s responsibility and is therefore out of scope for the SuD’s core flows.
  • The push-notification provider is not yet selected. The design reserves a message-driven NotificationService seam and stores a customer mobileNumber, but the concrete SMS/push integration is deferred to the future enhancement.
  • A relational database is the system of record; the JPA mapping (single-table inheritance for bills) assumes an RDBMS with adequate support for the chosen Hibernate dialect.
  • Disputes are routed to an available agent by an existing work-allocation mechanism; the design places the disputed bill into an agent workspace abstraction rather than implementing agent scheduling.
